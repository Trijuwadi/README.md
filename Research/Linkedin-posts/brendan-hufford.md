## Post 1 — [April 13 , 2026]

[Last year, I rebuilt the newsletter function for 6 B2B SaaS companies.
By breaking every "rule" the newsletter gurus swear by👇

- Nobody tracked clicks
- Nobody A/B tested a subject line
- Not a single “We're excited to announce..." in sight
- No “here’s our latest 3 blogs + a webinar”

My only rule: whatever the number of emails you think you need to write - delete half of them.

Most B2B co’s think to send newsletters regularly, they have to write them regularly.

Wrong.

Recycling 50% of our newsletter content INCREASED engagement (and cut our workload in half).

It's called the "Immortal Newsletter" strategy:

50% is timeless, high-performing content on rotation
50% is fresh, trending insights

Evergreen + ephemeral (revolutionary, I know)

+ Zero subscriber complaints about "repeat" content
+ 2x faster to produce
+ Higher read rates than our previous "100% fresh" approach

Weird what happens when you stop reinventing the wheel every Tuesday.

After 12 months of testing with Growth Sprints clients (and doing it myself for 3+ years), I feel like an idiot for doing it the old way.

Most subscribers don't read every single newsletter. So they're actually missing your best content.

Get off the newsletter hamster wheel. It was always optional.

PS - I'm thinking of sharing the exact system we use to select which content to recycle and how we rotate it (for free, no engagement ransom required). Want it?

Or keep letting your content calendar run your life. I'm not your dad]

## Post 2 — [April 14 , 2026]

[Bad newsletters take 30min to write. 
Good newsletters take 2+ hours.
Great newsletters also take 30min. 

How?

I don't need to tell you this, but most b2b newsletters aren't newsletters.

They're 3 blogs in a trench coat.

"Here's what we published this week."
"Don't miss our upcoming webinar."
"Check out our latest case study."

That's a marketing email.

Not a newsletter.

It takes 30 minutes to create.... but once your audience sees that, they stop opening it.

Because new data shows your audience hates marketing emails as much as they hate cold emails.

So you try to make it even better.

Take a lot of time on it. Write something original.

That takes hours.

Hours of focus you, honestly, don't have.

So here's how to start creating newsletters that actually get read:

I call it "The Operator Playbook"

1. Record your calls during the week
2. Pipe all of the recordings into Claude
3. Ask it to pull frameworks and templates you referenced (or create them based on what you talked about)
4. Upload your voiceprint
5. Draft a bad first draft
6. Punch it up for 30min
7. Done

Obviously there's a lot of nuance here, but that gets you started. This is the exact system I'm building for myself and clients right now.

(and yes, I know there's faster ways to do this with cowork, but we're trying to go 0 to 1, here)

Newsletters are meant to be a relationship loop - an ongoing marketing motion that makes you the default choice when your audience is in a buying motion.

## Post 3 — [April 17,2026]

Agency delivers a gorgeous strategy deck. 
SEO keyword exports.
Competitor research. 
Content calendars. 
The works.

Then... nothing happens, because:

- A new product feature launches
- The CMO gets pulled into a fire drill. 
- The deck sits in a Google Drive folder. 
- The one person who was supposed to execute it quits.

And I can empathize with ALL of that.

In house marketers are in a tough spot.

Modular content that actually ships. Real assets, not just slides.

Strategy still matters - but it happens while we're doing the work, not instead of it.
